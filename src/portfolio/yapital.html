Revolutionising Mobile Commerce

Yapital was the world's first cross-channel mobile payment app, allowing customers to pay online, in-store, via invoice and from street posters, all via QR-code. I introduced user-centric design practices, which led to a number of successes and product breakthroughs.

Introduction

Yapital had worked on its mobile payment solution for over a year before I joined. Unfortunately, at no point did the product teams test the technology or designs with prospective clients. And it showed. When they triumphantly went live, a whole host of problems began rearing their ugly heads. For me, it was quite the baptism of fire, but witnessing the amazing successes and cataclysmic errors of this enthusiastic Hamburg based start-up I learnt so, so much. The following gives some insight into the projects I worked on, my approach and learnings.

Kicking off User-Centred Design

Mobile payment was still the stuff of sci-fi back in 2012. It was no wonder then, that upon launch of their mobile payment app, the benefits of QR-code based mobile payments was viewed with skepticism, caution and even disbelief. The design of the apps, marketing and onboarding showed little awareness, let alone competence in assuaging the perceptions held by their target audience. On top of this, the product and service was riddled with problems caused by a complete lack of customer awareness and unchecked biases.

The Brief

Introduce user-centred design (UCD) to Yapital, and work with the company's product teams in Hamburg and Kiev to integrate UCD practices into their product development cycle.

The Process

I decided to use persona research and formation as a visible ‘entry strategy’. It's an approach that can be quite engaging, and my hope was that this very visual, collaborative exercise would begin raising awareness and enthusiasm.

Kick-Off presentation at company meeting

UCD is not just a design framework, it’s a mentality that needs to be widely adopted. I decided make it as participatory as possible, to improve buy-in, and because I simply couldn’t do it alone. To raise awareness I presented UCD and benefits at a company meeting. This was received very well.

Some slides from the presentation.

Colleague Recruitment

I began recruiting those colleagues whom I thought would be effective 'change agents' from different departments to help. Management support from each department certainly made this process a lot easier. This was the ‘core’ team.

User Research

I started simply, by creating a basic interview script which could be used by my recruited colleagues to carry out discovery interviews with family, friends and acquaintances who fit our defined user segments. The questions were quite basic, and I offered a few tips, and demonstrated an interview through role-play.

1 month later we had 20 recorded interviews, of reasonable quality and depth. As the interviews were codified and analysed, insights were being informally fed back through the departments by colleagues. Which was extremely exciting to see.

Once the personas were fleshed out, with the aid of secondary market research, we officially introduced the 3 consumer personas and 2 merchant personas to all our colleagues, which are now being used during design processes and prioritisation meetings.

A Nation-Wide Diary Study

Nobody in the world had yet paid at a brick 'n' mortar store's point of sale (POS) using a mobile phone. When trialling our payment service at Germany's biggest supermarket chain REWE, we found that staff were nervous, and Yapital customers were experiencing everything from childish excitement to utter embarassment.

The brief

In partnership with REWE, we were tasked with gathering insights into Yapital’s User Experience, with REWE’s POS as focus point.

The Process

To meet the expectations of our partners, and ensure the process was as fruitful as possible for both parties, we decided to carry out a nation-wide diary study, using an online platform, so that both Yapital and REWE could monitor the process and input from participants.

30 participants from all around Germany were recruited to take part, careful to ensure a representative sample, with regards to sex, city of residence, age and operating systems used.

The participants were set tasks over the course of three weeks. These were:

  1. Inform yourself about Yapital using their website.
  2. Onboard with Yapital (browser or app)
  3. Complete two purchases, on two separate days with Yapital at a REWE supermarket of your choice

Throughout the study, participants diligently completed the tasks and were surprisingly active on the forum, where we posed questions related to the tasks.

The Results

Needless to say, the 76 page report as a result of this 3 week process was an absolute goldmine, with regards to user experience insights. Though a number of concerns were brought to our attention, or confirmed, the overall feedback gave us good reason to believe that mobile payment was a future people were excited by, and with the right design approach could be successfuly adopted.

Unfortunately, much of the results are still covered by a Non-Disclosure Agreement.

In-store till payments

The Brief

I was asked to investigate if it was possible to remove the confirmation screen at in-store POS without adversely affecting the user-experience. This could significantly increase the speed of payment, but initial thoughts was that it was risky, as it provided reassurance and consistency with other payment flows.

The Approach

It was important to ensure that changing the user-flow at POS wouldn’t adversely affect people’s expectations in other user-flows, where the confirmation screen would be very necessary.

To test this I planned to test with 2 groups of shoppers. One would go through the checkout procedure without the confirmation screen first, followed by an invoice payment with the confirmation screen. The other group was reversed.

The test took place ‘guerilla style’ at a local supermarket. As I got volunteers, I introduced Yapital, explained the test and asked them how they would expect the payment process to function before both tests. With a keynote prototype the volunteers did a mock-payment, which gave an excellent impression of the real user-flow.

I then compared the reactions of both groups when the confirmation screen was left-out. Those who did an invoice payment first, and therefore were aware of the confirmation step, were not confused or worried when this step did not show up at POS. In fact neither group in pre interview expected a confirmation step. The flow was simply compared 1:1 with a card payment - and card payments due to having no UI cannot add this step.

The Results

We removed the confirmation screen for POS transactions without a single complaint. Checkout time decreased thanks to both human and system factors. Our supermarket partners were thrilled because now Yapital payments could consistently beat card payments for speed.

This speed improvement increased REWE's, trust decreased their skepticism, and directly led to their decision to roll out Yapital at more stores as part of a nation-wide trial.

Cashing up

The Brief

Early feedback from our merchants revealed that cashing-up at night was a pain, because to get the day’s revenue they had use their computer, which was often far away from other traditional till equipment. Instead, they wanted a way to quickly and easily do this via the app.

The Approach

I contacted a few of our smaller merchants in Hamburg and invited myself to their place of business, to observe their cashing-up procedures. I made notes of which information they required from till receipts, how it was noted, and how end-of-shift calculations were made.

Though Yapital is an electronic-money solution, with no traditional clearing processes, the merchant’s mental models were still very much based around traditional cash-registers. I made a note of the terminology they used when referring to the reports.

I called Business Analysts, Devs and Design colleagues together. I presented my research, shared quotes and prioritised the information needs. 2 hours later we had a complete paper-prototype which fulfilled our initial requirements. A couple of hours after that I had a click-dummy made up in Balsmiq that our devs were happy with.

I loaded this onto an iPad and I user-tested it with our merchants that week, informally, at their place of work. It was simple, but the task success rate was 100% with all 7 merchants.

The Results

Within just one sprint, our iOs and Android app developers were able to implement the designs. This was largely because they'd been a part of the design process and had a good understanding of the problem and solution. Once we had updated the merchant app with the new cashing-up functionality the feedback was brilliant. All it took was an understanding of existing mental models and processes shared by smaller merchants and a collaborative design process over the space of just a few days.
