Evolving Mobile Trading

IG is an industry leader in retail trading and I was asked to develop a design vision and strategy for the evolution of their mobile trading platforms.

Highlights

I led a research and design process, supported by a talented team of UI, dev and QA colleagues in London and Krakow. Our efforts led to the discovery of valuable user insights, numerous user experience improvements, and a validated long-term design direction for the future development of all IG's mobile trading platforms.

  1. Existing value was unlocked, by revamping the IA and navigation to really focus on the needs of traders while completing key tasks. This IA tested so well with clients it has become the template for all IG's trading platforms, and laid the foundation for a more cohesive experience upon which to build future functionality going forward.
  2. Key tasks were made up to 2 times more efficient thanks to the introduction of new interaction patterns and the addition of more supportive, less ambiguous information at key decision points.
  3. A new approach to discovery was validated. 'Dumb lists' were replaced with smarter, context rich collections, organised in a way that traders found useful and engaging. This has resulted in a whole new product category, aimed at more proactively helping traders research and find opportunities.
  4. The future cost of mobile design was drastically decreased thanks to a cross-platform approach from the very beginning that's resulted in UX and UI that scales across platforms while respecting mobile OS idiosyncrasies.

The Brief

The eagerly awaited 2015 annual Investment Trends Report had arrived. For the first time ever, IG's flagship app had slipped from the top spot... to third. IG had invested heavily in their apps the previous two years, making them by far the most feature-packed on the market, but this 'more is more' approach obviously wasn't resonating with clients.

With competition increasing and mobile trading becoming ever more important to clients, a serious strategic rethink was needed.

Objectives

  1. Identify why IG's apps are losing ground to competitors and better understand what traders are looking for in a mobile trading experience.
  2. Propose a validated set of forward-looking designs and prototypes that will inform how IG's trading apps should evolve over the next 18-24 months.
  3. Align the mobile apps with the visual identity forged by the recently released desktop trading app, to aid a consistent multi-platform trading experience.

Benchmark and Iterate

The first task was to begin benchmarking the current design and user experience, so we'd have something to measure proposed improvements against. To do this, I first carried out a thorough heuristic evaluation of the apps. The framework I hung my work on was David Travis' Red Route model , which defines a key task as one that's completed by all users, is high frequency, and is critical for both users and the business. It's straight-forward, and I find it great for getting user-centric, and naturally more business-centric stakeholders positively engaged with the process. Which is half the battle in design.


The Red Route tasks we identified, and confirmed with research were:

  1. Find a trading opportunity
  2. Create or edit an order
  3. Monitor positions

Having completed the evaluation, a few concerning conclusions stuck out.

Firstly, all three tasks were hindered by an inefficient feature, rather than task, oriented IA. This meant valuable functionality and features that aided a common task were often inaccessible from one another.

Secondly, creating an order, perhaps the most critical task in the app was far from optimised, or supportive of the task. Accessing the ticket often required several stages of navigation. Completing the ticket was not especially intuitive, contained ambiguous data, required more manual steps than necessary, and did not support differing client mental models and trading strategies.

Finally, iOS and Android looked and felt like they were built by different companies. Navigation, task-flows, UI and even labels were inconsistent. This, meant scaling optimal solutions for both would be impossible in the current state.

This last point I began tackling straight off the bat with developers by creating an OS specific style-guide and component library for both platforms. Though this didn't address the major problems and inconsistencies, as that would require more fundamental changes and a more robust research approach, it did improve UI consistency and overall aesthetics of the apps. This gave us a better foundation, on which to gradually make those more fundamental changes.

Research

Stakeholder Interviews

With the initial evaluation complete, I began talking with stakeholders around the business. My main aim was to understand the perspectives and objectives different areas of the business had for the apps, and learn about any current concerns, future hopes or existing ideas that'd influence the design going forward. To do this, I conducted semi-structured interviews with members of the Trading Services team (front-line client support) and various heads of departments.

It became clear that some of my concerns were shared by trading services, but those concerns were played down by major stakeholders. Their focus was on building new things. Even acknowledging existing problems was problematic in fact - particularly with the order ticket. They didn't have any problem themselves after all. Clearly some work had do be done.

Qualitative Research

Feeling well oriented, it was time to begin tackling the more robust research tasks that would help me form hypotheses, which would form the foundation of the design process later on.

The main questions I wanted to answer were:

  • How do traders find trading opportunities? What sources do they use, which data points are most important and which tools do they love?
  • How do traders go about creating orders? What factors are they weighing up, what informs trade sizes, how are they managing risk and what difficulties do they have translating strategy and intention to input?
  • How do traders monitor their accounts when they've "skin in the game"? How frequently do they check, what tools do they use and what frustrations have they faced in the pursuit of maximising profits and minimising losses?

To answer these questions, 3 main methods were used.

  1. User interviews, in which I spent up to 90 minutes with clients who frequently trade on mobile. This was a mixture of semi-structred interviews and having the clients demonstrate how they complete tasks currently.
  2. Watching usability testing of the recently launched desktop app. Though this didn't highlight mobile specific problems, it was very valuable in understanding how traders thought, where their priorities lay and their behaviours.
  3. Focus groups, as suggested by the research team. Despite the drawbacks of teasing insights from group-settings, focus groups aren't to be entirely dismissed if not used in isolation. They're useful primarily for identifying avenues of further research and a convenient opportunity to expose a team to clients in real time. So I helped the research team prepare this.

Quantitative Research

In parallel, I spent a lot of time getting data from B.I. (Business Intelligence, a data analysis tool), Trading Services support emails and whatever analytics the mobile teams could give me. This uncovered some nuggets of info that informed new design directions, or entirely new avenues.

One nugget was the highly frequent, but very short session times of 1-10 seconds. We hypothesised that traders were checking position and account balance information, because no navigation took place. This prompted us to investigate ways of surfacing this information outside of the apps, so traders could glean this with faster and with much less effort.

Another nugget was the discrepancy between the number of markets a trader might have in a watchlist, compared to the number they would trade. Currently, markets in watchlists and positions had exactly the same size and data density. Clearly we could look into making more optimised views for positions, considering the fact significantly fewer would ever be on the screen at the same time.

Once we'd sorted, categorised and analysed the data, we had a wealth of insights and research deliverables to work with. The outcomes included a set of trader personas/archetypes detailing characteristics to design for, and numerous hypothoses to begin exploring.

Workshops, London and Krakow

If we are not only to "get the right design" but also "get the design right" when implemented, building shared understanding of the project's goals, user insights, and plan of action is essential.

I planned and facilitated workshops with the mobile development teams in both London (iOS) and Krakow (Android). I and the researchers shared all insights and conclusions from the research process, which generated great, in-depth discussions. The product team members could begin taking an informed and active role in the design process from then on. We then discussed the Red Routes and how we would collaborate on the next steps.

Insight. Involving the rest of the mobile product teams only once much of the research had already been conducted isn't my preferred way of working. Far from it, but despite the PO's understanding for whole team involvement, the prevailing project-management culture was against us. You have to pick your battles, and this was a compromise.

Prototyping and Testing

With a good amount of research, and everyone on the same page, it was time to roll up our sleeves. The aim was to explore and validate the hypotheses formed in step 3 for each of the Red Routes. We wanted to produce a good-quality, thorough prototype for Premium client testing, which would help uncover new insights and inform iterative improvements... or total rethinks.

For each of the red routes, I and the mobile lead UI designer conducted semi-structured design sprints. Though we unfortunately weren't able to involve the rest of the mobile product teams' as much as we would have liked 'officially' (due to other running project commitments) we did involve them at every opportunity. We even went so far as to fix time outside of work hours to discuss design with the wider team. This is how imporant I believe developer and QA involvement in design is, even if that's not supported by official processes. The fact my team members agreed is testament to their commitment, and further proof there's an intrinsic motivation to collaborate at this level.

One major obstacle we had in this process was the same one shared by anyone designing in niche markets - access to clients for usability testing. This reality meant usability testing with real target clients was saved until we had explored all 3 sprints.

To help mitigate the issues that arise when you don't have the ability to frequently check assumptions, we turned to colleagues from around the business, particularly Trading Services team members. These colleagues are often active traders themselves, and they have first hand experience with clients day in day out. Though they'd have different biases, or be blind to certain IG-centric things such as terminology that could cause problems, their experience and intuition helped a lot. It's not perfect, but this definitely helped improve the design.

The steps for redesigning and prototyping each task

1. Map the problem. We mapped out the task, to clearly show the beginning and end points for each, and any interstitial steps we knew needed to be included. Often there were multiple possible start and end points, depending on the type of trader. For example, the way chartists identify opportunities and place trades is quite different from the flow a fundamental trader would follow.

2. Gather relevant data, insights and hypotheses. We walked through each step of the problem and attached all the insights we had for that step. This might be data insights, usability test observations, or trader characteristics - like mental models certain types of traders were assessing tasks with. This would help us go over the designs, as we reviewed each iteration's ability to solve or improve problems. More importantly, it would help others' see the inputs we had, and with their perspectives, help us improve, or cover our blind-spots.

3. Sketch and review. We sketched out ideas and I began drafting them up into wire-flows. These are low fidelity, easily changeable depictions of each screen involved in a task, showing interactions within each, and between each screen. I would add notes to the screens too, pointing out problems I was facing, or justifications for any design decisions I had made. This work was frequently reviewed by design colleagues during design crits, and by trading services.

4. Prototyping. Together with the UI designer we took the wire-flows and went slightly higher fidelity in Framer. This allowed us to sense check the data and layouts in the wireframes, and ensure each responded well to different device sizes - including tablet. If these basic things break down, we catch them early. Once we were happy, we plumbed in some real data from IG's API which really tested the layout, as we got real market names and prices in place.

5. Testing. Throughout the sketch and prototyping process, I identified the hypotheses that posed the most risk to the overall design - which included new navigation patterns that if failed, would undermine many other experiments. For this reason, we ensured we got concrete validations of these first. I worked closely with our internal researcher to script, record and evaluate each of the 15 sessions we had scheduled face-to-face with existing premium clients, and prospects.

Obviously the results from just 15 clients isn't the most robust of findings to pin major redesign decision on. So, once we were confident we had validated certain design approaches as best we could, I found a low risk approach to testing them in production with a larger number of traders. I introduced them to our mobile web apps. These have a much smaller user base, so I used them as a test bed, supported by the design teams and POs of our web apps. Being web-based, it meant we could release changes to gradually increasing numbers, and roll back if we spotted problems, far quicker. We tracked user behaviour and feedback, and found the designs held up.



That said, problems did become apparent. In some areas we'd pushed the design too far, to the point some steps within a task became unfamiliar. Fortunately, the potential adjustments were numerous and we were confident it was just a case of iterating, rather than concern for the entire direction.

Results

It was a huge amount of work, but extremely rewarding. Some of the bigger bets really paid off, risky patterns proved far less worrisome that worst case scenarios imagined, and clients reacted very positively to ideas in the prototype - despite it being in a very early phase.

We ended with incredibly valuable insights which validated some hypotheses, and totally invalidated others. The side effect of this approach was also a more tight-knit, more informed and empathetic mobile product team enthused about the upcoming work.

Over the coming weeks we iterated on these ideas, and folded testing into other research initiatives. The results of which were introduced towards the beginning of this case study.
